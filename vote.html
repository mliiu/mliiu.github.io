LA Vote Site: Card Sort Redesign





The purpose of this user research study is to propose a redesign for the information architecture of the Los Angeles voting website. Card sorting, open-card specifically, asks participants to organize topics into groups that make sense to them and then name each group they created in a way that accurately describes the content. The cards provided are based off scraping the current website for content material.

20.6% of eligible voters cast ballots in the primary election in LA County—651,392 were cast at vote centers and 485,631 were mail in ballots. A goal would be to promote more in mail ballot voting for people and create awareness of voting information and deadlines, after many people faced long wait times at in-person polling centers back in March 2020 due to technical problems.

How can we make voting information more accessible to promote voter education?

Research Plan     Survey     Instructions    


Procedure




10 participants were asked to complete the card-sorting activity and fill out a screener and pre-survey questionnare. Screening required participants to be an LA County Resident and eligible to vote (18+ years old).

Average age: 33.3, median age: 29.5, standard deviation: 12.87
Gender: Female: 30%, Male: 70%
Education: Associate Degree: 10%, Bachelor’s Degree: 80%, Master’s Degree: 10%


Standardization Grid



Since each participant labeled the categories they sorted the cards in, a standardization was necessary to merge categories with similar names and cards into one. The categories created appear on this grid, with how many times each card was placed within it. A darker cell color means more people sorted the card into a similar category (i.e. Community & Voter Outreach Committee was placed into “Other Programs” nine times) Scroll to see more



Similarity Matrix

A similarity matrix shows which cards the participants paired together in the same group the most often. The darker the color where 2 cards intersect, the more often they were paired; on a scale of 0-100, the latter means the 2 cards are always paired together. For example, “Interactive Sample Ballot” and “E-Sample Ballot Example” at the top of the chart are 100, meaning they are always grouped together. “Past Elections” and “Register to Vote” are at 9, with a low pairing score.


Dendogram


Due to the small sample size, the Best Merge Method was the more insightful dendrogram option. The tree makes assumptions about larger clusters based on pair relationships showing up to which points participants agree with parts of the grouping. The shorter the branch grouping, the more correlated.



“Voting Period Information” and “Voting in LA County” were commonly grouped together, and secondly grouped with “Military & Overseas Voting”. Contrastingly, “Multilingual Services Program” was least paired with the Ballot Information, indicated by the connected branch furthest away.
Post-Survey Questionnare




Participants answered some follow-up questions after the activity they just completed. When asked about their grouping strategy when organizing the cards, 1/3 of participants looked for keywords to define similarities and differences. 2/3 of participants sorted based from the perspective of site visitors, considering who would be looking at the content. In terms of expectations, the most common themes participants were expecting to see on a voting site were information regarding how to register to vote (100%) and where and how to vote (100%). Information on candidates & officials and election & ballot, were less of a priority but still important (60%).
